Point 1: Derive the condition for asbsolute decoupling

We have absolute decoupling when GDP increases while CO2 emissions decrease. Therefore, in terms of growth rates we have that: gr(GDP)>0 while gr(CO2)<0. Moreover, since gr(CI) = gr(CO2) - gr(GDP), where CI is the Carbon Intensity of GDP CI = CO2/GDP, we have that gr(CI)<0 since gr(GDP)>0 and gr(CO2)<0. Therefore, we have that: gr(GDP)>0 and gr(CO2)<0 => gr(CI)<0. The final result is that: |gr(CI)|>|gr(GDP)|

I observe relative decoupling if the dark blue line (CO2PC growth) consistently stays below the dark red line (GDPPC growth): in relative decoupling, the growth rate of CO2 per capita is less than the growth rate of GDP per capita. Moreover, the dark green line (CI growth) should be consistently below 0 (in relative decoupling we have negative growth for CI). This is the case for the majority of the time period, except from the sub-period 1990-1995 and around 2010. Therefore, there is some evidence of relative decoupling. I observe absolute decoupling if the dark blue line (CO2PC growth) consistently stays below 0 (negative growth) while the dark red line (GDPPC growth) consistently stays above 0 (positive growth): in absolute decoupling, GDP per capita increases while CO2 per capita decreases. This is not the case for CO2PC growth. Therefore, I conclude that, since there is no evidence of absolute decoupling because the CO2PC line is not consistently below zero but there is some evidence of relative decoupling , then my analysis shows some evidence of relative decoupling in Poland between 1990 and 2015, but not for the entirety of the time period.
